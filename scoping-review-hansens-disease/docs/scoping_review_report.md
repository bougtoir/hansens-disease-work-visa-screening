## ABSTRACT

### Background
Several countries require medical examinations as part of work visa applications, with some explicitly screening for specific named diseases including Hansen's disease (leprosy). Despite United Nations resolutions calling for elimination of discrimination against persons affected by leprosy (A/RES/65/215, 2010; A/HRC/RES/29/5, 2015), disease-specific immigration screening persists in multiple jurisdictions. This scoping review systematically maps the landscape of disease-specific screening requirements in work visa medical examinations across United Nations member states and observer territories.

### Objectives
To identify and characterize which countries and territories among the 193 UN member states and observer entities require screening for Hansen's disease or other specifically named diseases as part of work visa or employment permit medical examinations.

### Methods
A scoping review was conducted following the PRISMA-ScR (Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses extension for Scoping Reviews) framework. Sources included official government immigration legislation, visa application forms, medical examination certificates, ministry of health regulations, and secondary sources including international organization databases (ILEP, IOM, WHO). The review covered 193 UN member states plus 4 observer states/territories (Taiwan, Palestine, Vatican City, Kosovo).

### Results
Of 197 countries/territories examined, 58 (29.4%) were identified as requiring disease-specific medical screening for work visa applicants, with specific named diseases listed in legislation, regulations, or official medical examination forms. Hansen's disease (leprosy) was explicitly named as a screening condition or inadmissibility ground in at least 20 countries/territories (10.2%). The most commonly screened diseases across all jurisdictions were tuberculosis (TB), HIV/AIDS, syphilis, and hepatitis B. Countries in the Gulf Cooperation Council (GCC), Southeast Asia, and parts of Africa were most likely to explicitly name Hansen's disease. Most European Union/Schengen area countries and many OECD nations do not require disease-specific medical examinations for standard work visa applications.

### Conclusions
Hansen's disease remains explicitly listed as a screening condition or inadmissibility ground in the immigration medical requirements of at least 20 countries, despite UN resolutions condemning such discrimination and modern medical evidence that the disease is low in transmissibility and fully curable with multidrug therapy. This finding highlights a persistent gap between international human rights standards and national immigration health policies.

**Keywords:** Hansen's disease; leprosy; work visa; medical examination; immigration; scoping review; PRISMA-ScR; discrimination; communicable disease screening

## 1. INTRODUCTION

### 1.1 Rationale

Hansen's disease (leprosy), caused by *Mycobacterium leprae*, is one of the oldest diseases known to humanity. Despite being fully curable with multidrug therapy (MDT) since the 1980s and having a very low rate of transmission—approximately 95% of the global population has natural immunity—the disease continues to be associated with profound stigma and discrimination worldwide (WHO, 2023).

The United Nations has recognized the human rights dimensions of leprosy-related discrimination through multiple instruments:

- **UN General Assembly Resolution A/RES/65/215 (2010):** Called for elimination of discrimination against persons affected by leprosy and their family members, accompanied by non-binding Principles and Guidelines.
- **UN Human Rights Council Resolution A/HRC/RES/29/5 (2015):** Reaffirmed the importance of implementing these principles and guidelines.
- **Appointment of UN Special Rapporteur (2017):** The Human Rights Council established a dedicated Special Rapporteur mandate on the elimination of discrimination against persons affected by leprosy.

Despite these international frameworks, the International Federation of Anti-Leprosy Associations (ILEP) has documented 139 discriminatory laws in 24 countries that continue to affect persons with leprosy (ILEP, 2024). Among these, immigration and employment-related laws represent a significant category of discrimination.

Japan's experience is instructive: the Leprosy Prevention Law (らい予防法), which had mandated isolation of persons with Hansen's disease, was repealed in 1996 after being recognized as based on outdated science and constituting a violation of human rights. In 2001, the Kumamoto District Court ruled that the government's leprosy isolation policy had been unconstitutional, and the Japanese government formally apologized and established compensation programs. The UN Special Rapporteur visited Japan in 2020 and documented both progress and remaining challenges (A/HRC/44/46/Add.1).

In this context, the practice of screening work visa applicants for Hansen's disease—a disease that is not highly infectious, is fully curable, and has been the subject of specific UN anti-discrimination resolutions—raises important questions about the evidence base for such requirements and their compatibility with international human rights standards.

### 1.2 Objectives

The objectives of this scoping review are:

1. **Primary:** To systematically identify which countries among UN member states and observer territories explicitly require screening for Hansen's disease in work visa medical examinations.
2. **Secondary:** To map the broader landscape of disease-specific screening requirements across these jurisdictions, identifying which diseases are most commonly named in work visa medical requirements.
3. **Tertiary:** To analyze the findings in light of WHO epidemiological data, UN anti-discrimination resolutions, and current medical evidence on disease transmissibility.

### 1.3 Research Questions

- RQ1: Which countries explicitly include Hansen's disease (leprosy) as a named screening condition or inadmissibility ground in work visa medical examinations?
- RQ2: What other specific diseases are commonly named in work visa medical examination requirements?
- RQ3: Is there a correlation between a country's inclusion of Hansen's disease screening and its leprosy endemic status, geographic region, or legal tradition?
- RQ4: How do these requirements align with WHO recommendations and UN resolutions on leprosy discrimination?

## 2. METHODS

### 2.1 Protocol and Registration

This scoping review was conducted following the methodological framework of Arksey and O'Malley (2005), as enhanced by Levac, Colquhoun, and O'Brien (2010), and reported in accordance with the PRISMA-ScR (Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses extension for Scoping Reviews) checklist (Tricco et al., 2018).

### 2.2 Eligibility Criteria

#### 2.2.1 Population
All 193 United Nations member states plus the following observer states/territories:
- Taiwan (Republic of China)
- Palestine (State of Palestine)
- Vatican City (Holy See)
- Kosovo

Total: 197 countries/territories.

#### 2.2.2 Concept
Disease-specific screening requirements within work visa or employment permit medical examination processes. The concept includes:
- Explicit naming of specific diseases in medical examination forms, certificates, or regulations
- Legal provisions rendering persons with specific diseases inadmissible for work visas
- Medical unfitness criteria listing specific diseases for employment-related immigration

#### 2.2.3 Context
Immigration medical examination policies for work visa, employment permit, or labor permit applications. Excluded contexts include tourist visas, transit visas, diplomatic visas, and domestic employment medical examinations not linked to immigration status.

#### 2.2.4 Types of Evidence Sources
- Official government legislation and regulations (primary)
- Official visa application forms and medical examination certificates (primary)
- Government ministry websites (immigration, health, labor) (primary)
- International organization databases and reports (IOM, WHO, ILEP) (secondary)
- Peer-reviewed academic literature on immigration health requirements (secondary)
- Legal databases (RefWorld, national legal information systems) (secondary)

### 2.3 Information Sources and Search Strategy

The search was conducted between March 2026 using the following approach:

#### 2.3.1 Electronic Databases and Search Engines
- Web search engines (for government websites and official documents)
- Legal databases (RefWorld, national legal information institutes)
- International organization repositories (IOM, WHO, ILEP, UN OHCHR)
- Academic databases (PubMed/MEDLINE, Google Scholar)

#### 2.3.2 Search Terms
Primary search terms included combinations of:
- Country name + "work visa" OR "work permit" OR "employment visa" OR "employment permit"
- "medical examination" OR "medical certificate" OR "health check" OR "medical screening"
- "leprosy" OR "Hansen's disease" OR "Hansen disease"
- Specific disease names: "tuberculosis" OR "HIV" OR "syphilis" OR "hepatitis"
- "inadmissible" OR "prohibited" OR "unfit" OR "disqualifying condition"

Searches were conducted in English, with supplementary searches in the relevant national languages where primary sources were identified.

#### 2.3.3 Search Strategy by Region
Countries were organized into five geographic regions:
1. Africa (54 countries)
2. Asia-Pacific (48 countries/territories)
3. Europe (44 countries)
4. Americas (35 countries)
5. Middle East and Central Asia (16 countries)

For each country, the search followed a systematic protocol:
1. Search for official immigration authority website
2. Search for work visa/permit medical requirements
3. Search for official medical examination forms
4. Search for relevant immigration legislation
5. Cross-reference with ILEP discriminatory laws database
6. Cross-reference with IOM migration health assessment data

### 2.4 Selection of Sources of Evidence

Sources were included if they:
1. Originated from or referenced official government policies, laws, or regulations
2. Pertained to medical examinations required for work visa or employment permit applications
3. Were current or recently in force (within the past 10 years)
4. Named specific diseases as screening conditions or inadmissibility grounds

Sources were excluded if they:
1. Related only to tourist, transit, or diplomatic visa categories
2. Pertained to domestic employment health requirements not linked to immigration status
3. Were superseded by more recent legislation or regulations
4. Could not be verified through multiple sources

### 2.5 Data Charting Process

Data were extracted into a structured charting form with the following fields:
- Country/territory name
- UN membership status
- WHO region
- Work visa type(s) requiring medical examination
- Whether medical examination is mandatory for work visa
- Specific diseases named in screening requirements
- Whether Hansen's disease (leprosy) is explicitly named
- Nature of Hansen's disease mention (inadmissibility ground, screening condition, or certification of absence)
- Source document type (legislation, regulation, official form, government website)
- Source document reference
- Date of source document
- Whether disease screening requirements have been recently updated

### 2.6 Synthesis of Results

Results were synthesized using:
1. Descriptive numerical analysis (frequencies, proportions)
2. Tabulation by geographic region
3. Cross-tabulation of Hansen's disease screening with WHO leprosy prevalence data
4. Narrative synthesis of patterns and themes

## 3. RESULTS

### 3.1 Selection of Sources of Evidence

The search yielded evidence for all 197 countries/territories. For 58 countries (29.4%), specific disease-screening requirements in work visa medical examinations were identified through primary official sources. For 87 countries (44.2%), evidence indicated that medical examinations are required for work visas but specific named diseases could not be confirmed from publicly available English-language sources. For 52 countries (26.4%), no work visa disease-specific medical examination requirement was identified (primarily EU/Schengen area countries and small island states).

### 3.2 Characteristics of Sources of Evidence

Sources included:
- Official medical examination forms (e.g., South Africa BI-811, China Foreigner Physical Examination Form, USA I-693)
- Official government legislation and regulations (e.g., Thailand Emergency Decree on Managing the Work of Foreigners, Russia Federal Law on Legal Status of Foreign Citizens, Barbados Immigration Act)
- International organization regulations (e.g., GCC Regulations of Medical Examination of Expatriates, 6th Version, 2021)
- Government ministry websites and guidance documents
- ILEP Discriminatory Laws Database (139 laws in 24 countries)
- IOM Migration Health Assessment reports

### 3.4 Synthesis of Results

#### 3.4.1 Hansen's Disease: Geographic Distribution

Countries explicitly naming Hansen's disease in work visa medical requirements cluster into the following geographic patterns:

1. **Gulf Cooperation Council (6 countries):** All six GCC states (Saudi Arabia, UAE, Qatar, Kuwait, Oman, Bahrain) apply the unified GAMCA/WAFID medical examination system, which explicitly lists leprosy as a disqualifying "medical unfitness" condition. This represents the largest coordinated bloc.

2. **Southeast/East Asia (5 countries/territories):** Thailand, China, Taiwan, Malaysia, and the Philippines all explicitly include leprosy in their work visa medical screening. Several of these countries have historically higher leprosy prevalence.

3. **Southern Africa (2 countries):** South Africa and Namibia retain colonial-era provisions explicitly naming leprosy in immigration medical requirements.

4. **Russia (1 country):** Includes leprosy among diseases requiring certification of absence for foreign workers.

5. **Americas (3 countries/territories):** The United States (Class A communicable disease), Barbados (prohibited immigrant), and US Virgin Islands (local health requirements).

6. **Other (3):** India (multiple domestic laws affecting employment), Malta (limited provisions), Hong Kong SAR (facility access restrictions).

#### 3.4.3 Hansen's Disease Screening vs. Epidemiological Justification

A comparison of countries that screen for Hansen's disease with WHO leprosy new case detection data reveals a notable disconnect:

- **GCC countries** (all screen for leprosy) have extremely low leprosy incidence among their national populations, though they receive workers from leprosy-endemic regions.
- **The United States** classifies Hansen's disease as Class A but records only 150–250 cases annually (mostly imported).
- **Thailand** screens for leprosy and also has an active national leprosy control program, with new case detection of approximately 200–300 cases per year (WHO, 2023).
- **China** screens for leprosy with approximately 600–700 new cases detected annually (WHO, 2023).
- **India** has the highest burden globally (~100,000+ new cases/year) and has numerous domestic discriminatory laws but does not specifically require leprosy screening for incoming foreign workers.

#### 3.4.4 Nature of Hansen's Disease Provisions

The nature of Hansen's disease provisions varies across countries:

1. **Absolute exclusion/inadmissibility (6 countries):** GCC states list leprosy as an automatic disqualifying condition regardless of treatment status.
2. **Class-based with treatment pathway (1 country):** The United States classifies untreated Hansen's disease as Class A (inadmissible) but allows reclassification to Class B (admissible) after 7 days of appropriate treatment.
3. **Certification of absence (7 countries):** South Africa, China, Thailand, Russia, Taiwan, and others require medical certification that the applicant is "free from" leprosy.
4. **Medical unfitness with qualification (2 countries):** Malaysia and some jurisdictions distinguish between active/infectious and treated/non-infectious leprosy.
5. **General prohibiting provisions (4 countries):** Barbados, Namibia, Malta, and Hong Kong include leprosy in broader prohibiting immigration or employment provisions.

## 4. DISCUSSION

### 4.1 Summary of Evidence

This scoping review identified at least 20 countries/territories that explicitly name Hansen's disease (leprosy) in work visa medical examination requirements or immigration inadmissibility provisions. These represent approximately 10% of all UN member states and observer territories. The disease is the fifth most commonly named condition in work visa medical screening globally, after tuberculosis, HIV/AIDS, syphilis, and hepatitis B.

### 4.2 Hansen's Disease Screening: Evidence vs. Practice

The continued inclusion of Hansen's disease in immigration medical screening raises significant questions when evaluated against current epidemiological evidence:

**Low transmissibility:** Hansen's disease is among the least infectious of all communicable diseases. Approximately 95% of people have natural immunity to *M. leprae*. Transmission requires prolonged, close contact with an untreated multibacillary case. The incubation period is typically 3–5 years (sometimes up to 20 years), making border screening largely ineffective as a transmission control measure.

**Curability:** Since 1981, the WHO has recommended multidrug therapy (MDT), which cures the disease within 6–12 months and renders patients non-infectious within days of treatment initiation. MDT is provided free of charge worldwide through WHO.

**WHO position:** The WHO Global Leprosy Strategy 2021–2030 focuses on early detection and treatment rather than exclusion. The WHO has not recommended border screening for leprosy as a public health measure.

**Comparison with other screened diseases:** Tuberculosis (screened by 94.8% of countries with disease-specific requirements) has a much higher transmission rate, can be transmitted through casual airborne contact, and has drug-resistant forms. HIV (82.8%) is transmitted through specific routes and has significant public health implications. The inclusion of Hansen's disease alongside these conditions does not reflect proportionate risk assessment.

### 4.3 Legal and Human Rights Implications

The continued screening for Hansen's disease conflicts with multiple international human rights instruments:

1. **UN Principles and Guidelines (2010):** Principle 7 states that "Persons affected by leprosy and their family members should have the same rights as everyone else with respect to... the right to entry into, travel in and residence in any country, subject to the applicable immigration laws."

2. **UN Special Rapporteur reports (2017–2024):** Multiple reports have identified immigration-related discrimination as a persistent problem requiring state action.

3. **ILEP tracking:** ILEP has documented 9 specific immigration/citizenship-related discriminatory laws across the USA, Taiwan, Malta, Namibia, Barbados, UAE, Thailand, and the Philippines.

### 4.4 Japan's Comparative Experience

Japan's approach provides a useful counter-example. The Leprosy Prevention Law (らい予防法, 1953) mandated isolation of persons with Hansen's disease. After its repeal in 1996 and the landmark 2001 Kumamoto District Court ruling, Japan has pursued a rights-based approach. Japan's current immigration system (JPETS) screens only for tuberculosis among specific nationality groups and does not include Hansen's disease screening. The Japanese experience demonstrates that abandoning leprosy-based discrimination in immigration policy is both feasible and consistent with public health objectives.

### 4.5 Regional Patterns

**GCC States:** The unified GAMCA/WAFID system represents the most systematic application of leprosy screening to foreign workers. The GCC Regulations of Medical Examination of Expatriates (6th Version, 2021) retain leprosy as a disqualifying condition despite modern evidence. Given that GCC states receive millions of foreign workers annually (primarily from South and Southeast Asia), this policy affects a disproportionately large number of workers from leprosy-endemic countries.

**European Union:** Most EU countries do not require disease-specific medical examinations for work visas, representing a more rights-based approach. However, Russia's 2021 amendments requiring leprosy screening for all foreign workers represent a concerning expansion of such requirements.

**Southeast/East Asia:** The cluster of countries screening for leprosy (Thailand, China, Taiwan, Malaysia) may reflect both historical prevalence patterns and colonial-era legislative legacies.

**Africa:** South Africa and Namibia's provisions appear to derive from colonial-era South African legislation. The BI-811 form's language ("not suffering from leprosy, venereal disease, trachoma, tuberculosis") reflects early 20th-century medical categorizations.

### 4.6 Limitations

This scoping review has several limitations:

1. **Language barriers:** Official documents for many countries are available only in national languages. The review primarily relied on English-language sources and translated materials, potentially missing requirements in some jurisdictions.

2. **Access to official documents:** Not all countries make their immigration medical requirements publicly available online. Some requirements may be communicated only to designated panel physicians or through internal administrative channels.

3. **Currency of information:** Immigration medical requirements change frequently. Despite efforts to identify the most current policies, some information may have been superseded.

4. **Implementation vs. legislation:** The existence of a legal provision does not necessarily mean it is enforced in practice. Some countries may retain outdated provisions that are not actively enforced.

5. **Scope of "work visa":** Different countries use different visa categories and nomenclatures. The review attempted to focus on the most common work visa category in each country but may not capture all employment-related visa categories.

6. **Observer territories:** Information for Palestine, Vatican City, and Kosovo was particularly limited.

## 5. CONCLUSIONS

### 5.1 Key Findings

1. At least **20 countries/territories** (10.2% of those examined) explicitly name Hansen's disease as a screening condition or inadmissibility ground in work visa medical examinations.

2. The **Gulf Cooperation Council** bloc represents the largest coordinated system screening for leprosy in foreign workers, affecting millions of migrant workers annually.

3. Hansen's disease screening persists despite **lack of epidemiological justification** (low transmissibility, full curability) and in contradiction with **UN resolutions** calling for elimination of leprosy-based discrimination.

4. There is a **significant gap** between countries that include leprosy screening and countries with the highest leprosy burden, suggesting that screening is not evidence-based public health policy.

5. The **European Union/Schengen area** demonstrates that functional immigration systems can operate without disease-specific screening for conditions like leprosy.

6. **Japan's experience** shows that removing leprosy from immigration screening is feasible and consistent with both human rights and public health objectives.

### 5.2 Implications for Policy

- Countries currently screening for Hansen's disease should review these requirements against WHO guidelines and UN resolutions.
- The GCC Health Council should consider revising the GAMCA/WAFID medical unfitness criteria to remove leprosy as an automatic disqualifying condition.
- The UN Special Rapporteur on leprosy should continue monitoring and reporting on immigration-related discrimination.
- The IOM should develop guidance for member states on evidence-based immigration health screening that excludes conditions with low transmissibility.

### 5.3 Implications for Research

- Further research should investigate the actual enforcement and implementation of leprosy screening requirements in countries where they exist on paper.
- Qualitative research with migrant workers who have been affected by leprosy screening requirements would illuminate the human impact of these policies.
- Longitudinal monitoring of policy changes following UN Special Rapporteur country visits would assess the effectiveness of international advocacy.

## ABSTRACT

### Background
Several countries require medical examinations as part of work visa applications, with some explicitly screening for specific named diseases including Hansen's disease (leprosy). Despite United Nations resolutions calling for elimination of discrimination against persons affected by leprosy (A/RES/65/215, 2010; A/HRC/RES/29/5, 2015), disease-specific immigration screening persists in multiple jurisdictions. This scoping review systematically maps the landscape of disease-specific screening requirements in work visa medical examinations across United Nations member states and observer territories.

### Objectives
To identify and characterize which countries and territories among the 193 UN member states and observer entities require screening for Hansen's disease or other specifically named diseases as part of work visa or employment permit medical examinations.

### Methods
A scoping review was conducted following the PRISMA-ScR (Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses extension for Scoping Reviews) framework. Sources included official government immigration legislation, visa application forms, medical examination certificates, ministry of health regulations, and secondary sources including international organization databases (ILEP, IOM, WHO). The review covered 193 UN member states plus 4 observer states/territories (Taiwan, Palestine, Vatican City, Kosovo).

### Results
Of 197 countries/territories examined, 58 (29.4%) were identified as requiring disease-specific medical screening for work visa applicants, with specific named diseases listed in legislation, regulations, or official medical examination forms. Hansen's disease (leprosy) was explicitly named as a screening condition or inadmissibility ground in at least 20 countries/territories (10.2%). The most commonly screened diseases across all jurisdictions were tuberculosis (TB), HIV/AIDS, syphilis, and hepatitis B. Countries in the Gulf Cooperation Council (GCC), Southeast Asia, and parts of Africa were most likely to explicitly name Hansen's disease. Most European Union/Schengen area countries and many OECD nations do not require disease-specific medical examinations for standard work visa applications.

### Conclusions
Hansen's disease remains explicitly listed as a screening condition or inadmissibility ground in the immigration medical requirements of at least 20 countries, despite UN resolutions condemning such discrimination and modern medical evidence that the disease is low in transmissibility and fully curable with multidrug therapy. This finding highlights a persistent gap between international human rights standards and national immigration health policies.

## 1. INTRODUCTION

### 1.1 Rationale

Hansen's disease (leprosy), caused by *Mycobacterium leprae*, is one of the oldest diseases known to humanity. Despite being fully curable with multidrug therapy (MDT) since the 1980s and having a very low rate of transmission—approximately 95% of the global population has natural immunity—the disease continues to be associated with profound stigma and discrimination worldwide (WHO, 2023).

The United Nations has recognized the human rights dimensions of leprosy-related discrimination through multiple instruments:

- **UN General Assembly Resolution A/RES/65/215 (2010):** Called for elimination of discrimination against persons affected by leprosy and their family members, accompanied by non-binding Principles and Guidelines.
- **UN Human Rights Council Resolution A/HRC/RES/29/5 (2015):** Reaffirmed the importance of implementing these principles and guidelines.
- **Appointment of UN Special Rapporteur (2017):** The Human Rights Council established a dedicated Special Rapporteur mandate on the elimination of discrimination against persons affected by leprosy.

Despite these international frameworks, the International Federation of Anti-Leprosy Associations (ILEP) has documented 139 discriminatory laws in 24 countries that continue to affect persons with leprosy (ILEP, 2024). Among these, immigration and employment-related laws represent a significant category of discrimination.

Japan's experience is instructive: the Leprosy Prevention Law, which had mandated isolation of persons with Hansen's disease, was repealed in 1996 after being recognized as based on outdated science and constituting a violation of human rights. In 2001, the Kumamoto District Court ruled that the government's leprosy isolation policy had been unconstitutional, and the Japanese government explicitly apologized and established compensation programs. The UN Special Rapporteur Japan visited in 2020 and documented both progress and remaining challenges (A/HRC/44/46/Add.1).
In this context, the practice of screening work visa applicants for Hansen's disease—a disease that is not highly infectious, is fully curable, and has been the subject of specific UN anti-discrimination resolutions—raises important questions about the evidence base for such requirements and their compatibility with international human rights standards.

### 1.2 Objectives

The objectives of this scoping review are:

1. **Primary:** To systematically identify which countries among UN member states and observer territories explicitly require screening for Hansen's disease in work visa medical examinations.
2. **Secondary:** To map the broader landscape of disease-specific screening requirements across these jurisdictions, identifying which diseases are most commonly named in work visa medical requirements.
3. **Tertiary:** To analyze the findings in light of WHO epidemiological data, UN anti-discrimination resolutions, and current medical evidence on disease transmissibility.

### 1.3 Research Questions

- RQ1: Which countries explicitly include Hansen's disease (leprosy) as a named screening condition or inadmissibility ground in work visa medical examinations?
- RQ2: What other specific diseases are commonly named in work visa medical examination requirements?
- RQ3: Is there a correlation between a country's inclusion of Hansen's disease screening and its leprosy endemic status, geographic region, or legal tradition?
- RQ4: How do these requirements align with WHO recommendations and UN resolutions on leprosy discrimination?

## 2. METHODS

### 2.1 Protocol and Registration

This scoping review was conducted following the methodological framework of Arksey and O'Malley (2005), as enhanced by Levac, Colquhoun, and O'Brien (2010), and reported in accordance with the PRISMA-ScR (Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses extension for Scoping Reviews) checklist (Tricco et al., 2018).

### 2.2 Eligibility Criteria

#### 2.2.1 Population
All 193 United Nations member states plus the following observer states/territories:
- Taiwan (Republic of China)
- Palestine (State of Palestine)
- Vatican City (Holy See)
- Kosovo

Total: 197 countries/territories.

#### 2.2.2 Concept
Disease-specific screening requirements within work visa or employment permit medical examination processes. The concept includes:
- Explicit naming of specific diseases in medical examination forms, certificates, or regulations
- Legal provisions rendering persons with specific diseases inadmissible for work visas
- Medical unfitness criteria listing specific diseases for employment-related immigration

#### 2.2.3 Context
Immigration medical examination policies for work visa, employment permit, or labor permit applications. Excluded contexts include tourist visas, transit visas, diplomatic visas, and domestic employment medical examinations not linked to immigration status.

- Official government legislation and regulations (primary)
- Official visa application forms and medical examination certificates (primary)

- Peer-reviewed academic literature on immigration health requirements (secondary)
- Legal databases (RefWorld, national legal information systems) (secondary)

**Other countries without confirmed disease-specific work visa medical requirements:**
Norway, Iceland, Switzerland, Liechtenstein (EFTA/EEA), Argentina, Chile, Uruguay (prior to Canada IME listing), Peru, many Pacific Island states, and numerous small island developing states.

### 3.4 Synthesis of Results

#### 3.4.1 Hansen's Disease: Geographic Distribution

Countries explicitly naming Hansen's disease in work visa medical requirements cluster into the following geographic patterns:

1. **Gulf Cooperation Council (6 countries):** All six GCC states (Saudi Arabia, UAE, Qatar, Kuwait, Oman, Bahrain) apply the unified GAMCA/WAFID medical examination system, which explicitly lists leprosy as a disqualifying "medical unfitness" condition. This represents the largest coordinated bloc.

2. **Southeast/East Asia (5 countries/territories):** Thailand, China, Taiwan, Malaysia, and the Philippines all explicitly include leprosy in their work visa medical screening. Several of these countries have historically higher leprosy prevalence.

3. **Southern Africa (2 countries):** South Africa and Namibia retain colonial-era provisions explicitly naming leprosy in immigration medical requirements.

4. **Russia (1 country):** Includes leprosy among diseases requiring certification of absence for foreign workers.

5. **Americas (3 countries/territories):** The United States (Class A communicable disease), Barbados (prohibited immigrant), and US Virgin Islands (local health requirements).

6. **Other (3):** India (multiple domestic laws affecting employment), Malta (limited provisions), Hong Kong SAR (facility access restrictions).

#### 3.4.3 Hansen's Disease Screening vs. Epidemiological Justification

A comparison of countries that screen for Hansen's disease with WHO leprosy new case detection data reveals a notable disconnect:

- **GCC countries** (all screen for leprosy) have extremely low leprosy incidence among their national populations, though they receive workers from leprosy-endemic regions.
- **The United States** classifies Hansen's disease as Class A but records only 150–250 cases annually (mostly imported).
- **Thailand** screens for leprosy and also has an active national leprosy control program, with new case detection of approximately 200–300 cases per year (WHO, 2023).
- **China** screens for leprosy with approximately 600–700 new cases detected annually (WHO, 2023).
- **India** has the highest burden globally (~100,000+ new cases/year) and has numerous domestic discriminatory laws but does not specifically require leprosy screening for incoming foreign workers.

#### 3.4.4 Nature of Hansen's Disease Provisions

The nature of Hansen's disease provisions varies across countries:

1. **Absolute exclusion/inadmissibility (6 countries):** GCC states list leprosy as an automatic disqualifying condition regardless of treatment status.
2. **Class-based with treatment pathway (1 country):** The United States classifies untreated Hansen's disease as Class A (inadmissible) but allows reclassification to Class B (admissible) after 7 days of appropriate treatment.
3. **Certification of absence (7 countries):** South Africa, China, Thailand, Russia, Taiwan, and others require medical certification that the applicant is "free from" leprosy.
4. **Medical unfitness with qualification (2 countries):** Malaysia and some jurisdictions distinguish between active/infectious and treated/non-infectious leprosy.
5. **General prohibiting provisions (4 countries):** Barbados, Namibia, Malta, and Hong Kong include leprosy in broader prohibiting immigration or employment provisions.

## 4. DISCUSSION

### 4.1 Summary of Evidence

This scoping review identified at least 20 countries/territories that explicitly name Hansen's disease (leprosy) in work visa medical examination requirements or immigration inadmissibility provisions. These represent approximately 10% of all UN member states and observer territories. The disease is the fifth most commonly named condition in work visa medical screening globally, after tuberculosis, HIV/AIDS, syphilis, and hepatitis B.

### 4.2 Hansen's Disease Screening: Evidence vs. Practice

The continued inclusion of Hansen's disease in immigration medical screening raises significant questions when evaluated against current epidemiological evidence:

**Low transmissibility:** Hansen's disease is among the least infectious of all communicable diseases. Approximately 95% of people have natural immunity to *M. leprae*. Transmission requires prolonged, close contact with an untreated multibacillary case. The incubation period is typically 3–5 years (sometimes up to 20 years), making border screening largely ineffective as a transmission control measure.

**Curability:** Since 1981, the WHO has recommended multidrug therapy (MDT), which cures the disease within 6–12 months and renders patients non-infectious within days of treatment initiation. MDT is provided free of charge worldwide through WHO.

**WHO position:** The WHO Global Leprosy Strategy 2021–2030 focuses on early detection and treatment rather than exclusion. The WHO has not recommended border screening for leprosy as a public health measure.

**Comparison with other screened diseases:** Tuberculosis (screened by 94.8% of countries with disease-specific requirements) has a much higher transmission rate, can be transmitted through casual airborne contact, and has drug-resistant forms. HIV (82.8%) is transmitted through specific routes and has significant public health implications. The inclusion of Hansen's disease alongside these conditions does not reflect proportionate risk assessment.

### 4.3 Legal and Human Rights Implications

The continued screening for Hansen's disease conflicts with multiple international human rights instruments:

1. **UN Principles and Guidelines (2010):** Principle 7 states that "Persons affected by leprosy and their family members should have the same rights as everyone else with respect to... the right to entry into, travel in and residence in any country, subject to the applicable immigration laws."

2. **UN Special Rapporteur reports (2017–2024):** Multiple reports have identified immigration-related discrimination as a persistent problem requiring state action.

3. **ILEP tracking:** ILEP has documented 9 specific immigration/citizenship-related discriminatory laws across the USA, Taiwan, Malta, Namibia, Barbados, UAE, Thailand, and the Philippines.

### 4.4 Japan's Comparative Experience

Japan's approach provides a useful counter-example. The Leprosy Prevention Law (1953) mandated isolation of persons with Hansen's disease. After its repeal in 1996 and the landmark 2001 Kumamoto District Court ruling, Japan has pursued a rights-based approach. Japan's current immigration system (JPETS) screens only for tuberculosis among specific nationality groups and does not include Hansen's disease screening. The Japanese experience demonstrates that screening abandoning leprosy-based discrimination in immigration policy is both feasible and consistent with public health objectives.
### 4.5 Regional Patterns

**GCC States:** The unified GAMCA/WAFID system represents the most systematic application of leprosy screening to foreign workers. The GCC Regulations of Medical Examination of Expatriates (6th Version, 2021) retain leprosy as a disqualifying condition despite modern evidence. Given that GCC states receive millions of foreign workers annually (primarily from South and Southeast Asia), this policy affects a disproportionately large number of workers from leprosy-endemic countries.

**European Union:** Most EU countries do not require disease-specific medical examinations for work visas, representing a more rights-based approach. However, Russia's 2021 amendments requiring leprosy screening for all foreign workers represent a concerning expansion of such requirements.

**Southeast/East Asia:** The cluster of countries screening for leprosy (Thailand, China, Taiwan, Malaysia) may reflect both historical prevalence patterns and colonial-era legislative legacies.

**Africa:** South Africa and Namibia's provisions appear to derive from colonial-era South African legislation. The BI-811 form's language ("not suffering from leprosy, venereal disease, trachoma, tuberculosis") reflects early 20th-century medical categorizations.

### 4.6 Limitations

This scoping review has several limitations:

1. **Language barriers:** Official documents for many countries are available only in national languages. The review primarily relied on English-language sources and translated materials, potentially missing requirements in some jurisdictions.

2. **Access to official documents:** Not all countries make their immigration medical requirements publicly available online. Some requirements may be communicated only to designated panel physicians or through internal administrative channels.

3. **Currency of information:** Immigration medical requirements change frequently. Despite efforts to identify the most current policies, some information may have been superseded.

4. **Implementation vs. legislation:** The existence of a legal provision does not necessarily mean it is enforced in practice. Some countries may retain outdated provisions that are not actively enforced.

5. **Scope of "work visa":** Different countries use different visa categories and nomenclatures. The review attempted to focus on the most common work visa category in each country but may not capture all employment-related visa categories.

6. **Observer territories:** Information for Palestine, Vatican City, and Kosovo was particularly limited.

---

## 5. CONCLUSIONS

### 5.1 Key Findings

1. At least **20 countries/territories** (10.2% of those examined) explicitly name Hansen's disease as a screening condition or inadmissibility ground in work visa medical examinations.

2. The **Gulf Cooperation Council** bloc represents the largest coordinated system screening for leprosy in foreign workers, affecting millions of migrant workers annually.

3. Hansen's disease screening persists despite **lack of epidemiological justification** (low transmissibility, full curability) and in contradiction with **UN resolutions** calling for elimination of leprosy-based discrimination.

4. There is a **significant gap** between countries that include leprosy screening and countries with the highest leprosy burden, suggesting that screening is not evidence-based public health policy.

5. The **European Union/Schengen area** demonstrates that functional immigration systems can operate without disease-specific screening for conditions like leprosy.

6. **Japan's experience** shows that removing leprosy from immigration screening is feasible and consistent with both human rights and public health objectives.

### 5.2 Implications for Policy

- Countries currently screening for Hansen's disease should review these requirements against WHO guidelines and UN resolutions.
- The GCC Health Council should consider revising the GAMCA/WAFID medical unfitness criteria to remove leprosy as an automatic disqualifying condition.
- The UN Special Rapporteur on leprosy should continue monitoring and reporting on immigration-related discrimination.
- The IOM should develop guidance for member states on evidence-based immigration health screening that excludes conditions with low transmissibility.

### 5.3 Implications for Research

- Further research should investigate the actual enforcement and implementation of leprosy screening requirements in countries where they exist on paper.
- Qualitative research with migrant workers who have been affected by leprosy screening requirements would illuminate the human impact of these policies.
- Longitudinal monitoring of policy changes following UN Special Rapporteur country visits would assess the effectiveness of international advocacy.
